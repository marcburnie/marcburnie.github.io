Boot Camp Application Second Impressions

May 7th, 2020

I've officially been accepted into App Academy, Full Stack Academy, and Codesmith. After three weeks of learning about each program, I can easily put Codesmith as my top preference. They place higher importance on soft skills rather than technical know-how. Even so, their entrance requirements have also been the most technically challenging. They have an active online community which I've enjoyed participating in, and engaging lectures with great instructors.

I'll say in general though, that I initially viewed software boot camps with a lot of skepticism. Now I see boot camps as being a disruptive to the college/univeristy educational model. Having spent 6 years of my life earning my degrees and spending a small fortune on tuition, I've probably only used 1/10th of what I learned in university at my job. Everything I have learned during the boot camp application process is very relevant to what I want to do and the quality of the teaching and materials are comparable to the best courses I took in university. I think I would have been happy with any of the programs, but I am releived that I was accepted into Codesmith.

JavaScript First Impressions

Apr 29th, 2020

Thankfully I had more time to learn JavaScript than Ruby. Out of all the programming languages I have learned, it is the weirdest. It imitates many of the programming paradigms I've encountered, but operates completely differently. The best comparison I can think of is JS is a lot like a sleeper car; unassuming, powerful under-the-hood, and fragile if not used correctly. With that said, I don't think I've ever had as much fun learning a language since I first learned Visual Basic back in high school. I'm currently working through CSX which is Codesmith's free online JavaScript course, which seems to be very comprehensive. I'm finding it much more difficult than the Ruby coursework offered by App Academy; it's forcing me to look at other resources and participate in Codesmith's online community to reach out for help. This may sound like a bad thing, but this was my experience as a Mechanical Engineer as well. Engineering is difficult for good reasons and I'm enjoying the challenge.

Boot Camp Application First Impressions

Apr 25th, 2020

Well, I may be overreaching a bit with applying to 3 different boot camps. First of all, they all have their own entrance requirements. App Academy requires technical assessments in Ruby, while Full Stack Academy and Codesmith's assessments are in JavaScript. I knew nothing about either language last week, but because the cut-off for App Academy's next cohort application was yesterday, I know Ruby now! Luckily, I found Ruby very syntactically similar to Python, so it wasn't too difficult.

I really need to commend App Academy's application process. The first step involves completing a reading and logical assessment similar to a standardized English test and an IQ test. Both are timed, which I loathe, but I understand that it's probably the best way for App Academy to sort out anyone who doesn't have the capability to program without actually testing programming skills. I didn't feel I performed well myself, but luckily I did well enough to pass. Next, I had to complete their Introduction to Ruby coursework and pass an online written programming test in Ruby. The fact I was able to pick up Ruby in two days really speaks to the quality of the course material and exercises. Lastly, I had a remote technical interview yesterday that involved pair programming, which is also a new concept to me. Overall, I found the technical assessments to be quite easy, though I feel that anyone without a background in programming would struggle through even on a longer timeline. I should hear if I passed next week, but now I'll need to focus on my applications for Codesmith and Full Stack Academy. Onto learning JS!

Software Boot Camps

April 19th, 2020

The most common advice I've received about starting a career in Software Engineering is to enroll in a software boot camp. Apparently, boot camps have an established track record of employability and I've received a lot of anecdotal evidence to back this up. This is great news for me considering that most of these programs are a few months in length as opposed to a few years for a Software Engineering degree, so I've decided to go ahead and apply. The majority of boot camp programs are focused on full stack web development; while I had not decided on what area to specialize in, web development fits well with what I want to do and learn, and has optimistic job growth. However, there are a plethora of boot camps, and even deciding on which to apply to has been a challenge. I've narrowed the search down to three: App Academy, Full Stack Academy, and Codesmith. I selected these boot camps because Full Stack and Codesmith had the best employment outcomes on CIRR last year and App Academy because it showed up in both Course Report's and SwitchUp's top 5, though it was not transparent on employment outcomes on CIRR. Codesmith's outcomes were by far the most impressive: 92.1% employed in 180 days and $125K average starting salary in their NY program in H1 2019, which seems to good to be true. I guess someone else thought so because they were independently audited. First and foremost, my goal is employability as a Software Engineer, so my plan is to use the application period as a way to learn more about each without commiting. We'll see how it goes.

Onward and Upward!

Apr 18th, 2020

I was always told that the two best working days you'll have in your Schlumberger career is your first and your last. I can tell you from personal experience that, while this may not the case for everyone, the statement is true to me. Not to say I didn't enjoy my 10 years with the company, but I've always regretted that I did not pursue a career in software and automation. Now without the weight of responsibility that I had with Schlumberger, I am finally free to pursue a career that I want for myself.

I cannot tell you how excited I am to get back into software. Software Engineering and Computer Science were my dream careers as a teenager but, since I graduated around the dot-com boom and bust, I was always told there were no jobs. Ironic hearing that in 2020. Turns out that "follow your passion" is some good advice.

I'm expecting my transition to Software Engineering to be a challenging one since I'll need to prove I can do the job without ever having held the title or the degree. The last time I was in this situation I at least had my degrees and transcript to point to that demonstrated I had the requisite knowledge, but even then it didn't prove I could do the job. The advantage I have becoming a Software Engineer is that I can readily demonstrate what I know and what I can do - by building a website. This is why this site exists and, possibly, why you are here; to assess my competency. Regardless of the reason, I appreciate the time you have taken to explore my website and learn more about me. Feel free to reach out to me - I'd love to get some feedback on how to do things better or just knowing that someone actually visited a site I've put many hours into. Regardless, I hope you enjoy your time here and thanks for visiting. Onward and upward!
